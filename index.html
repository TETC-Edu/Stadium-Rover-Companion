Stadium Logistics Rover
Open CodeAIM ↗

Match day at NRG Stadium.

The 2026 World Cup is hours from kickoff. Three stations need supplies. Your autonomous rover is the only thing fast enough to make all three deliveries before the whistle.

3 cargo 3 zones 1 rover

What you're doing

You'll design your own stadium on the field mat, measure where everything is, then code the rover to make three deliveries on its own. You'll do this challenge three times — once with blocks, once with switch, and once typing real Python from scratch. Each round, you redesign the stadium, so the numbers in your code have to change.

The three deliveries

Soccer ball
→ Referee Station
Blue barrel
→ Team A Bench
Orange barrel
→ Team B Bench

Your field

A sample layout — yours will look different.

The arc

You'll do the same challenge three times. Each level adds one new thing:

  • Level 1 — Blocks. Drag block commands together. Make all three deliveries.
  • Level 2 — Switch. Convert your blocks to see the Python underneath. Edit numbers for a new field.
  • Level 3 — Python. Type real Python from scratch. Three deliveries, one after another.
  • Bonus — Loops & variables. Make your Level 3 code shorter and cleaner.
Before you code — Grab the controller and drive the rover by hand through one delivery first. Your hands need to know the field before your code does.

Level 1 — Blocks

Design your stadium. Measure it. Drive it by hand. Then code it in Blocks.

Blocks
Your task

Design your stadium layout. Measure the distance and turn for each zone. Then code the rover in Blocks to deliver all three cargo to the right zones.

Drive it first — Grab the controller and drive one delivery by hand before you touch any code. Get a feel for how the rover moves on your field.

Step 1: Design your stadium

Place your three zone markers on the field mat wherever you want. Place the rover at the supply room (the rover's starting spot).

One rule: each zone must be at least 30 cm from where the rover starts. Other than that, the layout is yours.

Step 2: Measure each zone

For each of the three zones, you need two numbers:

  • Distance. Use your ruler. Measure from the rover's center to the zone in centimeters, then multiply by 10 to get millimeters. (VEX wants mm.)
  • Turn. Use your protractor on the rover. Stand behind it and look where it's pointing. Is the zone to the left or right? How many degrees?

Write these numbers down. You'll need them in your code.

Step 3: Code it in Blocks

Open VEXcode AIM in Blocks mode. For each delivery, your stack will look something like:

  • Turn (left or right) by your measured degrees
  • Drive forward by your measured distance in mm
  • Kick (for the soccer ball) or Place (for the barrels)
  • Turn around and drive back to the supply room

Then do the same for zones 2 and 3. Run the whole stack.

Stuck? Ask a partner. Then ask me.

Open one card at a time. Each card has three hints. We give you the next one when you ask.

?The rover isn't going the right distance.
Try first Stop and measure again. Make sure you converted to millimeters. 1 cm = 10 mm. So 45 cm is 450, not 45.
Then this Run it once and watch where the rover actually stops. Is it short, too far, or pointing the wrong way? That tells you which number to change.
Approach Distance and turn are two different problems. Fix one at a time. Try driving straight (no turn) first — just see if you can get to the right spot. Then come back and fix the turn.
?The rover is turning the wrong way.
Try first Stand behind the rover. Look where it's pointing. Is the zone on its left side or its right side? That's the turn direction.
Then this Check the block. Did you pick Turn left or Turn right? Easy to grab the wrong one.
Approach Say it out loud with your partner first: "the zone is on the rover's right, so it turns right." If you can't say it, you can't code it.
?The kicker isn't firing.
Try first Is the Kicker block actually in your stack? It's easy to drop a Drive block where you meant a Kicker.
Then this The kicker has two modes: kick and place. The soccer ball gets kicked. The barrels get placed gently. Are you using the right one?
Approach Order matters. The rover has to arrive at the zone before the kicker fires. If it fires mid-drive, the cargo lands wherever the rover is at that moment. Walk through your stack one block at a time.

Pick up your three zone markers and put them somewhere new on the field. Don't be polite about it — really move them.

          Your task
          
Take your working Level 3 code and rewrite it using variables and a loop. Same three deliveries, same field, but the typing gets way shorter.

        


        

          
1. Spot the pattern

          
Look at the code you wrote in Level 3. You wrote three deliveries. What's the same every time? What's different?

          
Talk it through with your partner before reading on.


          

            The answer —
            The shape is the same every time: turn, drive, deliver, turn around, drive back. What's different is the numbers (distance, turn) and the cargo (kick vs place).
          

        


        

          
2. What's a variable?

          
A variable is a name for a value. Instead of writing 450 all over your code, you write distance, and you decide once what distance means.

          


# Before (number hardcoded)
robot.move_for(450, 0)

# After (with a variable)
distance = 450
robot.move_for(distance, 0)

          

          
That looks like more code, not less. But hold on — what if you had three different distances?

        


        

          
3. What's a loop?

          
A loop means: do this thing more than once. Instead of writing the same delivery code three times, write it once and tell the rover "do this for each delivery."

          
Think of your three deliveries as a checklist. A loop checks off each item, does the work, then moves to the next one.


          


# A list of three things
cargo_list = ["soccer_ball", "blue_barrel", "orange_barrel"]

# Do something with each one
for cargo in cargo_list:
    robot.screen.print(cargo)

          

          
This prints each cargo name one at a time. The loop does the repeating for you.

        


        

          
4. Build it

          
Now put it together. Each delivery has three things: a distance, a turn, and a cargo type. Stack them into a list.

          
Step by step:

          
    
            
  1. Make a list with your three deliveries' info: (turn_direction, degrees, distance, cargo).
    2. 
            
  2. Write the delivery code once, using variable names instead of real numbers.
    3. 
            
  3. Wrap that code in a for loop.
    4. 
          


          
Here's the shape — fill in your own numbers:

          


# Each delivery: (direction, degrees, distance_mm, cargo)
deliveries = [
    (RIGHT, 30, 450, "ball"),
    (LEFT,  45, 600, "blue"),
    (RIGHT, 90, 300, "orange"),
]

for direction, degrees, distance, cargo in deliveries:
    robot.screen.print(cargo)
    robot.turn_for(direction, degrees)
    robot.move_for(distance, 0)

    if cargo == "ball":
        robot.kicker.kick(MEDIUM)
    else:
        robot.kicker.place()

    # turn around and drive back
    robot.turn_for(direction, 180)
    robot.move_for(distance, 0)

          

          
Replace the numbers in the list with your real measurements. Don't touch the loop code below it.

        


        

          
5. The payoff

          
Remember Level 2, when you had to fix every number by hand on a new field?

          
With a loop, you'd only change the numbers in one place — the list at the top. The driving code never changes.


          

            That's the whole point —
            Variables and loops aren't fancy computer-science magic. They're what you do when you notice you're typing the same thing over and over. You had the problem first. Now you have the fix.
          

        


        

          
Stuck?

          


            

              

                
?My loop only runs once.

                
              

              

                

                  Try first
                  Count the items in your list. A loop runs once per item. If your list has one item, you get one run.
                

                

                  Then this
                  Check indentation. Every line inside the for must be indented the same (use 4 spaces). Lines that aren't indented are outside the loop.
                

                

                  Approach
                  Put robot.screen.print(cargo) as the first line inside the loop. Now you can see the loop run on the rover screen. If you see three names, the loop is fine and the bug is somewhere else.
                

                

              

            


            

              

                
?My variables are doing weird things.

                
              

              

                

                  Try first
                  Check the order. The names in the for line have to match the order of values in your list. If you wrote (direction, degrees, distance, cargo) but unpacked as distance, direction, degrees, cargo, your numbers are in the wrong slots.
                

                

                  Then this
                  Pick one delivery. Print all four values on the rover screen before the rover moves. If the screen shows numbers in the wrong slots, your list and your unpack don't match.
                

                

                  Approach
                  Comment out everything except one delivery in your list. Get that one running with variables. Then add delivery 2. Then delivery 3.
                

                

              

            


            

              

                
?The kicker fires for the wrong cargo.

                
              

              

                

                  Try first
                  Check your if line. What word are you comparing? Does it exactly match the word in your list ("ball" vs "soccer_ball")?
                

                

                  Then this
                  Two equals signs: ==, not =. One equals sign assigns a value, two equals signs checks if things match.
                

                

                  Approach
                  Print the cargo name right before the if. Look at the screen. Now check your if against what you just saw. They have to be exactly the same word.
